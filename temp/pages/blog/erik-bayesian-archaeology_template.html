Bayesian inference beyond chronology

ArchaeologyMethodsBayesianInference

Bayesian methods are often introduced in archaeology through radiocarbon chronology, and that makes sense. Chronological modeling is one of the places where Bayesian thinking has had the most visible impact. But the logic of Bayesian inference is much broader than that. It gives us a way to compare competing hypotheses, update our beliefs in light of evidence, and express uncertainty in a direct and interpretable form.

That broader view is what interests me most. In many archaeological settings, we are not simply trying to estimate a date. We are trying to decide whether one explanation is more plausible than another, whether a pattern is strong enough to support a behavioral interpretation, or how much uncertainty should remain after a particular line of evidence is considered. Bayesian inference is useful precisely because it treats those questions as inferential problems rather than as mechanical decisions.

This is one reason I think Bayesian archaeology should not be reduced to a niche method. It is a general way of thinking about evidence. It forces us to be explicit about prior expectations, transparent about model structure, and careful about how much confidence the data can actually support. That is as relevant to zooarchaeology, lithic analysis, and taphonomy as it is to chronology.

For students and collaborators, that also makes Bayesian work pedagogically valuable. It encourages a more honest conversation about uncertainty and helps make the relationship between theory, data, and inference much clearer. In that sense, Bayesian approaches are not just technical tools. They are part of a broader scientific style of reasoning.
