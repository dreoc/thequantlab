Climate, innovation, and the African Pleistocene record

ArchaeologyAfricaClimateHuman Evolution

Climate change mattered in the African Pleistocene, but it is rarely enough to explain archaeological change on its own. One of the recurring problems in this literature is the temptation to tell a simple story in which environmental stress produces innovation in a direct and predictable way. The archaeological record is more complicated than that, and the best interpretations are usually the ones that keep regional history and technological variability in view at the same time.

That is why I find debate useful here. When we compare different perspectives on innovation, climate, and human adaptation, the interesting question is not whether climate mattered at all. It obviously did. The harder question is how climate interacted with local landscape conditions, mobility, social transmission, and material practice. Innovation is not just a reaction. It is a process that unfolds within particular human communities and particular archaeological contexts.

Research in the Kalahari and related African contexts keeps reinforcing that point. The record shows moments of persistence, flexibility, and technological change, but those moments do not always line up with a simple climate trigger model. Sometimes the evidence points to continuity under changing conditions. Sometimes it points to selective uptake or uneven distribution of particular practices. Those patterns matter because they remind us that innovation has a social and archaeological history, not just an environmental backdrop.

For me, that is where the strongest work is headed. Rather than asking whether climate caused innovation in a general sense, we should ask what kinds of evidence would let us distinguish among competing models of change. That keeps the discussion empirical, comparative, and analytically useful.
